Proposal Abstract

Developing Ethical Decision-Making Frameworks for Pandemic Response

Thembinkosi KM Rushwaya - University of Johannesburg (2025-2029)

The COVID-19 pandemic catalysed a profound global crisis that exposed deeply entrenched ethical tensions within societies worldwide, by highlighting the tensions between safeguarding individual liberties and advancing public health, preserving economic stability and fulfilling social responsibilities, and reconciling national interests with global security imperatives. These tensions prompted ethical questions regarding the most appropriate guiding principles for decision-making in the face of such unprecedented challenges. A central question still looms: should pandemic responses incorporate the application of an integrated framework of virtue ethics, deontological ethics, or utilitarian values?

This study posits that an integrated, pluralistic ethical approach rather than adherence to any one singular framework – offers the most robust and morally defensible foundation for guiding decisions in public health crises. Through a systematic philosophical inquiry, this research seeks to reconcile and synthesize the core tenets of virtue ethics, deontological ethics, and utilitarianism, arguing for a comprehensive model of ethical decision-making that draws on the strengths of each perspective.

In synthesising these diverse ethical frameworks, this study proposes a multi-faceted ethical model that balances moral character, individual rights, and the pursuit of collective outcomes. The model advances that public health decision-making requires the careful calibration of these principles, acknowledging the moral complexities that were experienced in responding to crises like the COVID-19 pandemic.
